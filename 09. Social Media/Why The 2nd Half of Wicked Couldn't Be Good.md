# Big-Picture Notes

Fractal story: the outsider is abused by her family --> the outsiders are abused by society
myth: the cosmological sins of society can be overcome by a weak outsider who is granted the power to redeem society via a higher power but then must sacrifice herself (except she doesn't die lol)

# Schizo 2am bullet points

- the cosmological sin and karmic imbalance: Elphaba is mistreated as a child (harm is done to the innocent). her foundational desire is for acceptance, which she mischaracterizes as assimilation
- the slim chance: Elphaba goes to Shiz—but only to accompany her sister
- the sword in the stone: Elphaba reveals her power (which are implicitly justified by the moral metaphysics of the story) and is "chosen" 
- finally here / dream come true (promise of the premise / rising action): Elphaba moves into Shiz
- complications: Shiz offers difficulties beyond what Elphaba could have imagined (in the form of the very political and social complications that she has been granted this tremendous power for the purpose of undoing)
	- B-plot 1: "He chose her, not me": just as with social standing, Elphaba is ostracized romantically 
- initiation / proving yourself (false victory): Elphaba thrives despite prejudice ... and even wins over Glinda, gaining provisional acceptance into the (flawed) community
- midpoint / crisis: Elphaba has gained acceptance, but the other marginalized characters are deeply at risk. this is the first real fracture in the legitimacy of what Elphaba originally wants (assimilation)
	- B-plot 2: "help comes in unexpected places": the male romance lead ends up helping her with a small gesture to help one marginalized person—but it's abundantly clear that this effort (while morally sanctioned by the story's moral compass) is Not Enough
- the slim chance #2: deeply tied to the first slim chance. going to Shiz was in uncritical search of assimilation into society; going to the Emerald City is to challenge the underlying moral fabric of that society.
- the weighing of ideologies: the Wizard lays out the story's anti-theme (a society is an aesthetic construct, not a moral one, and it needs theatrics for stability, and theatrics demand a villainized "other")
- glimpse into the "bad ending" future: the Wizard manipulating Elphaba to use her powers to aid the state (which incidentally harms the marginalized "other") provides a tangible, emotionally salient reason why Elphaba cannot accept the Wizard's offer (the Wizard's anti-theme proposition, while clearly flawed, is not emotionally sufficient on its own to viscerally justify Elphaba turning her back on him for the audience)
- the irony and epiphany: the moment when Elphaba can finally have what she originally wanted is the moment when she can no longer accept it. note how the story layers personal arc (newfound flight = moral empowerment and agency), social arc ("I hope you're happy" represents abandoning the social circle she fought hard to join and the lingering doubt that accompanies this sacrifice) and cosmological arc ("look at her; she's wicked" ties into the question of whether it is more moral to seek stability at the cost of fairness or fairness at the cost of stability)

Act 2 sucks (and everybody knows it) because they turned what should have been the break into 3 (the moment before the climax) into the midpoint—but a midpoint is characterized by a falsity (false victory or false defeat) that forces the character to reconsider their original motivations and worldview. "Defying Gravity" is the most emotionally weighty moment in the entire musical, and its moral legitimacy means that it doesn't have the narrative machinery to function as a midpoint transition (which should be hermeneutic, flagging that something is wrong and/or unsustainable).

In a tighter dialectic structure, Elphaba's newfound flight would empower her to immediately challenge the Wizard's anti-theme (antithesis) claim, and the sacred/natural power granted to her by the story's moral compass would allow her ideology to triumph over the Wizard's technocratic power. Note how the story's aesthetic aversion to mediation (i.e., "this is just a knockoff of the original movie") becomes a moral aversion ("technology is evil because it seeks to supplant magic/nature via symbolism and reproduction"). Naturalistic power (magic) is implicitly aligned with a humanistic/life-affirming moral framework, whereas technocracy and industrialization thrive on inequality and mass-reproduction of nature and life (i.e., we are sold symbols of nature, love, sex, family, etc., and the greatest sins of the Wizard's technocratic state are his theatrics, of which his secret police, supremacism, false claim to magic, and disempowerment of vulnerable groups are only props)
